### [Daring Fireball: What Is a Dickover?](https://daringfireball.net/2026/05/what_is_a_dickover)

> The article by John Gruber on Daring Fireball introduces the term 'dickover' to describe modal panels, popovers, or curtains presented by websites or apps that deliberately obscure content to frustrate users with unwanted, unnecessary, mandatory interactions. These interactions often include requests to accept cookies, subscribe to newsletters, or agree to terms of service. Gruber argues that these 'dickovers' are pervasive on the web and mobile apps, likening them to a scourge that interrupts the user experience. He explains that while he initially called them 'dickpanels', the term 'dickover' better captures the irritation they cause. The article also distinguishes 'dickovers' from 'dickbars', which are less intrusive non-modal popovers. Gruber emphasizes that while all dickovers are modal blockers, not all modal blockers are dickovers, as some, like paywalls, are necessary.

#### AI generated summary

John Gruber's article defines 'dickover' as a term for intrusive modal panels on websites and apps that frustrate users with unnecessary interactions, such as cookie permissions or newsletter sign-ups. ## Security

### [npm left-pad incident - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Npm_left-pad_incident)

> The npm left-pad incident occurred on March 22, 2016, when programmer Azer Koçulu removed the 'left-pad' package from npm, a package manager for JavaScript, after a dispute with Kik Messenger over the 'kik' package name. This removal caused widespread disruption as thousands of software projects, including Babel and React, depended on 'left-pad'. The incident highlighted issues in the software industry regarding dependency management, the potential for supply chain attacks, and the influence of corporate interests on open-source software. In response, npm, Inc. restored the package and changed its policy to prevent similar incidents in the future.
